Prometheux lets you model your business as a **knowledge graph**, reason over it with rules, and trace exactly how every result was derived. This page explains the core building blocks and how data lineage is captured through the chase graph.

## Knowledge graph

A knowledge graph is a graph in which entities in your business (e.g. Users, Companies, Wallets) are connected to other entities via relationships.

For instance, user "Alice" "works at" the company "Acme Corp". "Alice" has a "Wallet" for storing currency.

Using a knowledge graph, you can find the amount of currency "Acme Corp" has by traversing the graph and summing up all the wallets of all users who work at "Acme Corp".

Typically, knowledge graphs are stored as triples: (Entity A, Relationship, Entity B). Prometheux doesn't force you to conceptualise your data in this way, allowing you to model relationships that involve multiple properties and entities.

## Core building blocks

### Data source

A data source is any structured or semistructured data that you wish to connect into a knowledge graph. See [Data Sources](/vadalog/data-sources) for the connectors and formats supported.

### Ontology

At the core of Prometheux is an ontology. This is the set of business rules that you write to define how the entities in your system relate to each other, and how entities are connected to your real data.

These rules are written in [Vadalog](/vadalog/overview).

### Data binding

Included in your ontology is a way to tell Prometheux which data sources power the entities in your ruleset. In Vadalog, there are specific [`@bind` and `@qbind` annotations](/vadalog/annotations) for binding your rules to your data.

### Reasoning

By processing the rules and data in your ontology, Prometheux is able to deduce new facts or specific outcomes by processing your rule program. This processing is known as **reasoning**.

## The chase graph: how results are derived

When you ask Prometheux to reason over a set of rules, it performs this processing by repeatedly _chasing_ the rules in your ruleset until their logical conclusion.

The steps it takes to reach an answer are known as a **chase graph**: a graph of the rules and data that contributed to the answer. This graph is itself a knowledge graph that can be explored and analysed, giving you full lineage and provenance over every derived fact.

For the full reference, see [Chase and Provenance](/vadalog/chase-provenance).

### Materializing the chase graph

Prometheux natively supports full explanations of logical processes for output generation in reasoning tasks via the materialization of the chase graph during its parallel and distributed evaluation.

The chase graph mode is activated using the `@chase` annotation and is materialized into data sources such as CSV files or Neo4j databases. Its syntax is:

```
@chase("datasource", "filepath", "filename").
```

For example, the following program stores the chase graph as CSV at `disk/data/chase.csv`:

```prolog
arc(1,2).
arc(1,3).

path(X,Y) <- arc(X,Y).
@chase("csv", "disk/data", "chase.csv").
@output("path").
```

The resulting CSV records each derived fact alongside the facts and rule that produced it:

```csv
Fact,ProvenanceLeft,ProvenanceRight,Rule
"path(1,2)","arc(1,2)","","path(X,Y) <- arc(X,Y)"
"path(1,3)","arc(1,3)", "","path(X,Y) <- arc(X,Y)"
```

The columns capture the chase fact, the left body predicate, the right body predicate (empty if the fact was generated by a linear rule), and the rule that generated it. This is the foundation for lineage: every output can be traced back through these derivations to the original source data.

<Tip>
The chase graph can also be materialized directly into Neo4j (or into CSV structured for Neo4j bulk import) to navigate very large lineage graphs interactively. See [Chase and Provenance](/vadalog/chase-provenance) for the Neo4j configuration, retrieval queries, and optimization tips.
</Tip>
